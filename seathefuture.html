Sea The Future

Promoting dialogue about rising levels within coastal communities in order to increase awareness of the possibility of climate adaptation measures.

Project Overview

Team Project

Contributions:

User Research, Ethnographic Research, Product Design

Client:

Dublin City Council

Sector:

Interactive Education, Public Awareness, Installation

Duration:

4 Weeks

Aim

Promoting dialogue about rising levels within coastal communities in order to increase awareness of the possibility of climate adaptation measures.

Researching the current perception of the public

Using a structured interview we walked up to 12 residents of a coastal area and asked them about rising sea levels and flooding experiences.
We found that:

People saw this as a far-away issue, not affecting Ireland.

Most people had no idea when the impact of rising sea levels will be felt in the area.

People believed city council had a plan, but did not know what plan they had.

People knew that continuous flooding would impact the community (schools, graveyards).

Design Principles

1.

Sensitivity

As we were tackling a controversial and painful topic which affects many areas of life, sensitivity was of importance. 

2.

A Tangible Embodied Interaction

The future of rising sea levels is an intangible idea. Our research told us that because you can’t see it, people don’t think about it. Our design has to make the subject matter relevant to people's everyday life.

3.

Conversational

As this is such a complex topic, Our design must encourage two way conversation, one-way communication is not suitable for fully understanding the aspects of rising sea levels.

We designed a framework for the city council to implement for a managed retreat.

Since our challenge mission was to promote awareness and dialogue about adaptation measures, we decided to focus on the first three: Trigger, Educate and Engage.

Rapid Prototyping

After ideating, we decided on something very simple that can be done quickly and will give us more insight on what a successful intervention in the coastal community would look like.  Using research through design, we integrated co-design activities that would Trigger, Educate and Engage.  

To intrigue people, we brought a big cardboard wall in which people could write “what I love about the area”. 

We asked people to circle where they live on the map and fill out how old they will be so they can visualise themselves in the year 2040. 

A flood prediction from floodinfo.ie (considering big emission cuts) was overlaid on top of the map to surprise and trigger.

What We Learned

People felt very strongly towards protecting their community and area. Their initial reaction was to cut commissions to prevent frequent flooding, but we told them that even with emission reduction, this is the prediction. We gathered and organised our insights from each activity into three categories: what worked, what didn’t work and ideas.

Scale up the map and make it eyecatching

Create an unmoderated Interaction

Make people realise sea level rise is inevitable

Make the interactions more fun

Catch younger adults attention

Getting creative:

The new design features a wheel with three sections: a plain map of the area, an annual flood prediction, and an annual flood prediction with severe emission cuts.

The minimal difference between the two flood predictions aims to highlight the inevitability of rising sea levels, prompting users to understand that the effects are unavoidable regardless of emission reductions.
